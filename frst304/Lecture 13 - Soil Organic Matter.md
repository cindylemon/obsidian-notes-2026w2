Healthy soils are an essential part of nature-based solutions to climate change
- Soil carbon pools are double what is in the plants global
	- If soil carbon is released to the atmosphere, it could be devastating
	- We lose about 60% of soil organic carbon when we clearcut
- Soils have degraded by about 1/3 overall due to human practices
- Healthy soils is essential to fighting climate change
- Carbon is always cycling

Porosity = Total Pore Space
- Pores are voids between soil particles that can be filled with water or air
- Impacted by soil texture
	- Corase/sandy soils have larger pores but less total pore space. This means rapid H2O infiltration, low moisture retention, well-aerated
	-  Fine/clay soils have smaller pores, and more pore space. This means slow H2O infiltration, high moisture holding capacity, leading to waterlogging
- Soil organic matter also holds water. It's spongey and sticky.
- Soil Structure is the permeability
	- Water moves through granular soil quickly
	- Blocky: big aggregates of soil, structured, less rapid water pereation, often made with organic matter. Most permeable.
	- Platy: clays, plates/pages of clay that water has to move around. Least permeable.
- Water moves straight through a sandy loam
- In a clay loam, it takes a longer time. It spreads out more and doesn't really go as far down

Soil Water
- Saturated soil has its pores are full of water
- Gravitaional water: moves down and out of the soil profile due to gravity. Generally larger pores.
- Capillary water: water held in the soil due to adhesion and cohesion against the pull of gravity (due to polarity). Usually in fine pores.
- Water that has left the soil can be measured by the amount of gravitational water that leaves the soil 
	- The tightly held water is AKA the field capacity
	- Wilting point is when all remaining water in the soil is held too tightly for nutrients to be taken up by plants

Soil Chemical Properties
- Soil solutions are soups of many dissolved chemicals in water available for plants and soil organisms
	- Cations (+): Al3, NH4, K, Mg, Ca
	- Anions (-): Cl, NO3, PO3, SO4, HCO3
- Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC) is the ability of soil to hold onto nutrients/cations
	- Good indicator of soil fertility, quality and productivity
- Clays and humus tend to be negatively charged overall, so they're good measures of soil fertility
