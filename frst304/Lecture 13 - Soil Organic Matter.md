Porosity = Total Pore Space
- Pores are voids between soil particles that can be filled with water or air
	- The water inside is like a soup
	- Water and air is needed by the stuff inside the soil
- Impacted by soil texture, as it impacts the air and water content
	- Corase/sandy soils have larger pores but less total pore space. This means rapid H2O infiltration, low moisture retention, well-aerated
	-  Fine/clay soils have smaller pores, and more pore total space. They hold This means slow H2O infiltration, high moisture holding capacity, leading to waterlogging. Absorbs water and holds it better
- Soil organic matter also holds water. It's spongey and sticky.
- Soil Structure impacts the permeability
	- Water moves through granular soil quickly (eg. grains, larger pore space, not alot of toruosity)
	- Blocky: big aggregates of soil, structured, less rapid water pereation, often made with organic matter. Most permeable.
	- Platy: clays, plates/pages of clay that water has to move around. Least permeable. Takes the longest to move through.
- Water moves straight through a sandy loam right to the bottom
- In a clay loam, it takes a longer time, and is more platy. It spreads out more and doesn't really go as far down

Soil Water
- Saturated soil has its pores are full of water
- Gravitational water: moves/drains down and out of the soil profile due to gravity. Generally larger pores.
- Capillary water: water held in the soil due to adhesion and cohesion against the pull of gravity (due to polarity). Adheres to the sides of soil particles/aggregates. Usually in fine pores.
- Water that has left the soil can be measured by the amount of gravitational water that leaves the soil after a massive rain event
	- The tightly held water is AKA the field capacity (fine pores are full but not dried out)
	- Wilting point is when all remaining water in the soil is held too tightly for nutrients to be taken up by plants. Plants will wilt because they can not remove the water

Soil Chemical Properties
- Soil solutions are soups of many dissolved chemicals in water available for plants and soil organisms
	- Cations (+): Al, NH4, K, Mg, Ca
	- Anions (-): Cl, NO3, PO3, SO4, HCO3
	- Positive charges are attracted to negative charges
- Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC) is the ability of soil to hold onto nutrients/cations
	- Constantly exchanging off and on clay and organic particles
	- Good indicator of soil fertility, quality and productivity
	- We don't want nutrients to leak out of the soil
	- Constantly changing cations on and off the structure and the solution
	- Low surface area means less opportunity for cations to exchange off that surface
- Clays and humus tend to be negatively charged overall, so they're good measures of soil fertility (Organic Soils)
- The bigger the charge, the tighter the hold.
	- Lower surface area means less opportunity for cations to exchange off that surface
	- Sands have low cation exchange capacity
