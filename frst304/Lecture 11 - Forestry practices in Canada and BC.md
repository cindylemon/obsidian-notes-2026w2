 **BC Forests right now**
- Forests of BC are currently exhausted
- Information on forests should be available to the public
- There have been millennia of occupation of people originating from Northeast Asian. Indigenous people have been in NA dating back to 17,000 years
-  Only 2-3% tall treed forests remaining
- 92% of forest harvesting in BC is clearcutting

**Reciprocity Principle**
- Respecting all living things, every relation
	- There is responsibility to the forest, life and culture comes from the forest
- Colonial view: we are separate from the environment. Taking, extracting. (eg. fur trade, sending things back to Europe)
- Indigenous view: Forests are sociocultural ecosystems

**History of Forest Management in Canada**
- The Tragedy of the Commons: Economic/environmental theory where individuals acting in their own self-interest overuse a shared, finite resource, depleting/destroying it for everyone.
	- Forests were a commons taken over by settlers
	- The systems were degrading because responsibility was taken away from original owners
- Forests are 93% Crown land, provinces had jurisdiction over the land management itself
- Stumpage: royalty a company pays to the govt to harvest trees 
- Different treaties have been made to recognize indigenous rights and title to their own land and resources
	- Very few treaties, as provinces want to keep resources for itself
- Timer harvested in BC has been rising, then plateau, then declining.

- 1912 first forest act
	- Anything that was financially viable to cut would be cut (harvesting all old growth and stuff basically), with the idea that portions would grow back in 100 years = maximum benefit to society
	- Conservation: full use of resource
- Rate of cut grew after WW2 in the 1940s
	- Rights of cuts would be more concentrated into corporations, rights of Indigenous and small businesses were cooked. This made more damage to the forest
	- Wanted to liquidate the old growth forest to create "normal forests": tree farms
		- Government made Timber Supply Areas (TSAs) cookie cutter sections of the province.
		-  there's a regular rate of cut from them after they determine rate of harvest, inventory, growth rate
		- Calculated amount of sustainable Annual Allowable Cut (ACC) based on what the forest will grow back (Goal is to generate as much revenue for Province/shareholders as possible)
	- Big cuts, big machines, clear cuts
- Things started to fall apart in the 1980s
	- Increase mechanization decreased jobs, there was also economic downturn
	- Logging occurred faster than forests can grow back
		- Forests naturally want to grow to 1-2k years old
		- Old growth/primary forests are much more valuable than the plantations that are replacing them
- 1990's, started harvesting more: "uplift of the cut"
	- Accelerated cutting to capture dead wood, selling it before it decayed on the stump
	- We need to recover, dismantle the timber supply area system, get rid of AAC, and manage/look after the forests

History of Silviculture Systems in BC
- Used to clearcut, then started clearcutting with reserves (leaving some stands of trees intact)
- Silviculture system: a program of treatments for a whole rotation
	- Selected to address objectives and constraints for the site
	- Pre-harvest prescription: what is that ecosystem like? Who is living there? What are the goals?
	- Regeneration cutting method: how it is harvested, how the site is prepared, and how it is regenerated
	- Post-regeneration treatments: brushing (weeding around), spacing (taking out extra trees), pruning (taking off lower branches), fertilization (overcome nutrient limitations), thin (pulling the wood out), pre-commercial thinning (thinning that you can't profit from)
- Clearcut: Removing a stand of trees >=1 hectare, >2 tree heights in width in a single harvesting operation. This establishes a new even-aged crop by either planting, reseeding, or advance generation. It's 50% in an open climate, not influenced by edge, and can include green tree or patch reserves (no more than 7% of the stands left)
	- We don't do reseeding because it will get eaten by small critters
	- The most efficient, lucrative, cheapest, cost-effective way to regenerate a forest after
- Patch cut system: little clearcuts that are <1 hectare in size. It creates a distinct even-aged stands by planting, seeding, or advance regeneration. for species that love shade.  Does not depend on shelter incidentally provided by the surrounding uncut stand. <50% open area climate
- Progressive clear cut: starting at one end of a valley, taking out whole valleys while building road

- Clearcutting with reserves is aka a variable retention system, borrowing from aspects of even aged forests. Specifically/aka an aggregate retention/group retention, as trees are aggregated in patches.
	- Variable retention: at least 50$ of the area has "forest influence"
	- Reduce ecological impact of harvesting by retaining elements of stand and forest structure
	- Reduces aesthetic impact of harvesting to better gain public acceptance
- Dispersed retention: taking out individual trees, leaving little gaps in the forest.
- Seed tree system: selected trees or tree groups left during harvest to provide a seed source for natural regeneration
	- Seed trees may or may not be left during harvest
	- Cut can be timed so that the trees are setting cone/dispersing seeds that same year
- Shelterwood system: thin stands but provides shelter and seed for the next stand. Intent is to provide shelter, seeds, release remaining trees from competition
- Selection systems/uneven-aged management: harvesting timber at specific repeated intervals
	- more appropriate for shade tolerant species
	- Creates an uneven, multistory complex structure that's like old growth

- Despite having access to all of these systems, we've mostly been using clearcutting and have a high rate of cut. If we do more local management with better care and stewardship, we can fill the big carbon deficit and fill it by 25-50%
